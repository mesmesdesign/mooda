**Dramaturgy od space**

After dark, the city becomes a space for a unique experience. During the day, social interactions, movement and the activities of its inhabitants form the dynamic fabric of urban life. Night, however, alters the way we perceive our surroundings. Limited visibility, silence and a changed atmosphere mean that public space begins to affect the senses in a different way than during the day. At this time, light, contrasts and elements of the space that guide the eye and set the mood of a place take on particular significance.


The city is a space where different cultures, aesthetics and ways of life meet. After dark, these differences often become more apparent, as light selectively highlights chosen fragments of the urban landscape. The result is a unique scenography of the night-time city, in which some elements are highlighted whilst others remain hidden in the shadows. In this context, public space ceases to be merely a functional arrangement of streets and squares – it begins to resemble a stage upon which the daily ‘theatre of the city’ is performed.
Traditionally, public space has been understood as a place of social dialogue, where the primary means of communication was the spoken word. However, the experience of the city is not limited to language. Images, atmosphere and the emotions evoked by the space play an equally important role. This is particularly evident after dark, when perception relies largely on visual impressions and the mood of the place.


In this sense, the city at night can be compared to a theatre stage. Thinkers of the Enlightenment era already saw in the theatre a model of public space in which social values and conflicts are revealed. Contemporary artistic practices often employ a similar logic, taking theatre beyond the walls of institutions and bringing it into the urban space. In such situations, the city itself becomes the stage, and its users – conscious or accidental participants in the event.


The role of the spectator also changes in the street space. In traditional theatre, the audience is situated in a clearly defined space. In the city, these boundaries become blurred. Passers-by become potential spectators of events, often without even planning to take part in them. It is the theatre that comes to the city, rather than the residents coming to the theatre. As a result, the relationship between the space, the actors and the audience becomes more spontaneous and open.


Urban space itself plays a key role in such an experience. Streets, squares, building façades and elements of infrastructure can serve as a natural backdrop. Their form, scale and spatial layout create a framework for the events that may unfold within them. After dark, the significance of these elements often changes. Lighting highlights selected architectural features, brings out the texture of materials and creates new relationships between light and shadow.


Illumination then becomes one of the most important tools for shaping the city’s night-time landscape. Properly designed lighting can guide the eye, build spatial tension or create a sense of security. At the same time, it allows us to discover familiar places in a completely new way. Objects that seem ordinary during the day can take on a sense of drama and mystery at night.


Darkness itself is an important element of the night-time urban experience. It naturally evokes various emotions in people – ranging from uncertainty and tension to curiosity and fascination. Upon entering a dimly lit space, people begin to analyse their surroundings more intently. Every point of light attracts attention and becomes a clue for the observer to interpret the scene.


For this reason, the contrast between light and shadow plays a huge role in the perception of the night-time environment. Thanks to chiaroscuro, it is possible to perceive the depth and structure of objects. Architecture becomes more vivid, and details that go unnoticed during the day can be clearly highlighted.


Unlike daylight, which falls from above and creates relatively predictable shadows, artificial lighting offers far greater scope for creating visual effects. Lamps can be positioned below eye level or directed at various angles. This method of lighting can alter the way architectural forms are perceived. In some cases, it can even reverse the impression of depth – convex elements then appear concave, and concave elements appear convex.
Such phenomena enhance the sense of mystery and the uniqueness of the night-time environment. Familiar places begin to look different, and their interpretation becomes more open. A similar effect is often used in theatre, film or photography, where light serves to build tension and atmosphere.


However, light in the urban environment serves not only an aesthetic function. It also has practical and navigational significance. Illuminated structures – bridges, distinctive buildings or street furniture – can act as landmarks. They help residents and visitors find their way around, whilst simultaneously building the city’s visual identity.
Contemporary lighting design increasingly takes the social aspect into account as well. Well-designed lighting can help revitalise public spaces and enhance a sense of safety. Bright, attractively lit places are more readily visited by residents, which fosters social integration and urban activity.


In this context, light becomes a tool for the revitalisation of space. It can bring forgotten parts of the city to the fore, highlight the historical value of architecture, and give a new quality to places that were previously overlooked. Night-time illumination is therefore not merely decoration – it is part of a broader process of shaping the experience of urban space.
Thanks to modern technologies, it is also possible to design dynamic lighting scenes. Lighting systems can alter the intensity, colour or direction of light, creating visual sequences that respond to events or the needs of residents. In this way, the city at night becomes a dynamic and living space, where light helps to shape the atmosphere of the place.


After dark, urban space ceases to be merely a backdrop to everyday activities. It becomes a sensory experience in which light, shadow and architecture create a shared narrative. It is then that the city reveals its theatrical character, and its users become participants in the nocturnal spectacle of space.
